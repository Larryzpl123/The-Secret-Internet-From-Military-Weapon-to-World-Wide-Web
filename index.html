The Secret Internet From Military Weapon to World Wide Web, 1940–2026

HI300 Final Project  ·  Larry Zhong  ·  Mr. Weiss  ·  Spring 2026

Background Overview

The internet was not invented by Silicon Valley garage hobbyists. It was funded by the United States Department of Defense, designed by Cold War strategists who believed a Soviet first strike could decapitate American command-and-control in under thirty minutes, and built in research labs whose paychecks came from the same Pentagon that built the hydrogen bomb. Between 1945 and 1969, the same federal apparatus that produced Hiroshima produced ARPANET—a "survivable" packet-switched network whose entire purpose was to keep the United States able to retaliate after a nuclear strike.

What makes the internet's history so strange is that this military weapon was given away to civilians almost casually. Von Neumann's foundational EDVAC report was leaked rather than patented. Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn's TCP/IP protocols were published openly. Tim Berners-Lee released the World Wide Web into the public domain in 1993. By the time the NSF shut down the federal backbone in 1995, the network had quietly slipped out of government hands and into the hands of ordinary people, who used it to do everything its founders never imagined—shop, gossip, organize protests, fall in love, and, eventually, run their entire economy through it.

This timeline tracks that transformation: how a classified weapon designed to outlive a nuclear war became the most consequential cultural object of the late twentieth century, and what its military origins still mean for the network we live inside today.
