The Secret Internet

From Military Weapon to World Wide Web
U.S. History HI300 · Semester 2 Final · Larry Zhong & Partner · May 2026

Background & Overview

The Cold War (1945–1991) was won not on battlefields but in laboratories and research universities. After the Soviet Union shocked the United States by launching Sputnik 1 in October 1957, the federal government poured unprecedented sums into computing, communications, and missile research, fearing that a single nuclear strike could decapitate the nation's command-and-control system.

Out of that Cold War paranoia grew the Advanced Research Projects Agency, whose engineers spent the 1960s designing a decentralized packet-switching network that could survive having its central nodes vaporized. That network — ARPANET — went live in 1969 with a four-line message between UCLA and Stanford that crashed after two letters. Half a century later, that same architecture, now called the internet, carries roughly ninety-six percent of all transoceanic data and underpins every aspect of modern life.

This timeline traces the thread from the smoke of Hiroshima to the privatized internet of the 1990s, showing how the world's most universal civilian technology was originally an unambiguous weapon of war. It then extends into 2025–2026, where renewed U.S.–China competition over semiconductors and artificial intelligence has made the network itself, once again, a strategic asset whose access governments openly try to control. The story of the internet is, fundamentally, a Cold War story — one that never really ended.
