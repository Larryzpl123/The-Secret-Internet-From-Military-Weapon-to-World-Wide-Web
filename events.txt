'1945.8.6'
"Atomic Bombs Dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki"
@importance 4
(On August 6 and 9, 1945, the United States detonated **two atomic bombs** over Japanese cities, ending the Second World War and unveiling a weapon that could erase a city in minutes.[footnote 1])
(For the next half-century, every American defense decision — including the design of the internet itself — would be made under a single grim assumption: //a Soviet first-strike could destroy the country's communications in under thirty minutes//. The whole story that follows is the engineering response to that fear.)
[footnote 1] Hersey, John. "Hiroshima." The New Yorker, August 31, 1946. The bomb's psychological legacy directly motivated the "survivable network" research that produced ARPANET twenty-four years later.

'1945.7'
"Vannevar Bush Publishes 'As We May Think'"
@importance 2
(Vannevar Bush — FDR's wartime science adviser and architect of U.S. military R&D during the war — publishes an essay in **The Atlantic** imagining the //Memex//, a desktop machine that lets users link and traverse information by association.[footnote 1])
(The Memex never existed, but its central idea — //a personal device for connecting any piece of information to any other// — is the conceptual ancestor of hypertext, the World Wide Web, and the modern search engine.)
[footnote 1] Bush, Vannevar. "As We May Think." The Atlantic Monthly, July 1945, 101–108.

'1947.12.23'
"Transistor Invented at Bell Labs"
@importance 2
(John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley demonstrate the first working transistor at **Bell Telephone Laboratories** in Murray Hill, New Jersey.[footnote 1])
(The transistor replaced the bulky, hot, fragile vacuum tube and made it physically possible to build computers small, cheap, and reliable enough to be deployed throughout a continent-spanning military network. //Without the transistor, there is no internet.//)
<>
{ARTIFACT — Replica of the first point-contact transistor, Bell Labs, 1947. Source: Nokia Bell Labs Archives.}
[footnote 1] Riordan, Michael, and Lillian Hoddeson. /Crystal Fire: The Birth of the Information Age/. New York: W. W. Norton, 1997.

'1957.10.4'
"USSR Launches Sputnik 1"
@importance 5
(The Soviet Union places the first artificial satellite into low Earth orbit. The 184-pound aluminum sphere broadcasts a radio "beep" that any American with a shortwave receiver can hear passing overhead every 96 minutes.[footnote 1])
(The political shock is immediate. If the Soviets can put a satellite over Washington, they can put a //thermonuclear warhead// there too. Within four months, President Eisenhower asks Congress to create a new agency to make sure the U.S. is **never again surprised** by a foreign technology — the agency that will, a decade later, build ARPANET.)
[footnote 1] McDougall, Walter A. /The Heavens and the Earth: A Political History of the Space Age/. New York: Basic Books, 1985, 134–156.

'1958.2.7'
"ARPA Created"
@importance 4
(President Eisenhower signs DoD Directive 5105.15, establishing the **Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA)** inside the Pentagon.[footnote 1])
(ARPA's mandate is broad — //fund whatever research keeps the United States ahead of the Soviet Union// — and its budget is enormous. Within a decade, ARPA money will fund packet switching, time-sharing, computer graphics, and the foundational AI labs at MIT, Stanford, and Carnegie Mellon. Every major piece of the internet's architecture is paid for by this single agency.)
[footnote 1] Hafner, Katie, and Matthew Lyon. /Where Wizards Stay Up Late: The Origins of the Internet/. New York: Simon & Schuster, 1996, 13–42.

'1962.4'
"Licklider's 'Galactic Network' Memos"
@importance 3
(J. C. R. Licklider, newly hired to run ARPA's Information Processing Techniques Office, circulates a series of internal memos addressed to his "Members and Affiliates of the **Intergalactic Computer Network**."[footnote 1])
(The memos describe a future in which //every computer in the country is connected to every other// and any user can access any program or dataset remotely. At the time, computers were room-sized objects that could not even communicate with the machine in the next room.)
(In the same year, on the other side of the Iron Curtain, Soviet cyberneticist Viktor Glushkov proposes **OGAS** — a nationwide computer network for managing the Soviet planned economy. It is killed by bureaucratic infighting and never built. A striking what-if for an alternate-history internet.)
[footnote 1] Licklider, J. C. R. "Memorandum For Members and Affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network." ARPA, April 23, 1963.

'1964'
"Paul Baran's Distributed Network"
@importance 3
(At the **RAND Corporation**, an Air Force-funded think tank, engineer Paul Baran publishes /On Distributed Communications/, a twelve-volume report describing how to build a communications network that survives a nuclear strike.[footnote 1])
(Baran's central insight: a network shaped like a //fishnet// — many small nodes, each connected to several others — has no central headquarters to bomb. Even with 50% of nodes destroyed, messages still get through by automatically rerouting. This is **packet switching**, the fundamental architecture of the internet to this day.)
<>
{ARTIFACT — Paul Baran's centralized / decentralized / distributed network sketch, 1964. RAND RM-3420-PR. One of the most reproduced diagrams in computing history.}
[footnote 1] Baran, Paul. /On Distributed Communications: I. Introduction to Distributed Communications Networks/. Santa Monica: RAND Corporation, August 1964. Document RM-3420-PR.

'1969.10.29'
"ARPANET Goes Online — The First Message"
@importance 5
(At **10:30 p.m. Pacific time**, UCLA graduate student Charley Kline types the word "LOGIN" to send the first message over ARPANET to a second computer at Stanford Research Institute. After two letters — //L, O// — the system crashes.[footnote 1])
(The first message ever sent on the ancestor of the internet is therefore the accidental, almost biblical word **"LO"**. The crash is fixed within an hour and the full message goes through the same night. The internet is, technically, born.)
<>
{ARTIFACT — IMP Log Book, UCLA, October 29, 1969, 22:30. The handwritten note "talked to SRI / Host to Host" is the official birth certificate of the internet. Source: Kleinrock Center for Internet Studies, UCLA.}
[footnote 1] Kleinrock, Leonard. "An Early History of the Internet." /IEEE Communications Magazine/ 48, no. 8 (August 2010): 26–36.

'1972.10'
"Email Invented; ARPANET Demonstrated Publicly"
@importance 3
(BBN engineer **Ray Tomlinson** writes a small program called SNDMSG that lets users on different ARPANET hosts send each other messages, separating the user from the host with the **@ symbol** — the only character on his keyboard not already used in usernames.[footnote 1])
(Three months later, ARPANET is demonstrated publicly for the first time at the **International Conference on Computer Communications** in Washington, D.C. The demonstration converts skeptics overnight; by 1973, ARPANET has crossed the Atlantic to nodes in Norway and London.)
[footnote 1] Tomlinson, Raymond. "The First Network Email." BBN Technologies, 1971. The choice of @ was, in his own words, //"obvious — there was no @ in any user's name."//

'1974.5'
"Cerf and Kahn Publish TCP"
@importance 3
(Vint Cerf (Stanford) and Bob Kahn (DARPA) publish "A Protocol for Packet Network Intercommunication" in the //IEEE Transactions on Communications//.[footnote 1])
(The paper describes **TCP** — Transmission Control Protocol — a single common language that lets //completely different networks talk to each other//. Without TCP, the internet would have remained a Pentagon-only affair. With it, any network anywhere could plug in. This is the moment the "inter-net" becomes possible.)
<>
{ARTIFACT — Cover page of Cerf & Kahn, "A Protocol for Packet Network Intercommunication," IEEE Trans. Comm. 22(5), May 1974. The paper is the technical Magna Carta of the internet.}
[footnote 1] Cerf, Vinton G., and Robert E. Kahn. "A Protocol for Packet Network Intercommunication." /IEEE Transactions on Communications/ 22, no. 5 (May 1974): 637–648.

'1983.1.1'
"TCP/IP 'Flag Day'"
@importance 4
(On **January 1, 1983** — known to network historians as "Flag Day" — every host on ARPANET is required to switch from the older NCP protocol to **TCP/IP**.[footnote 1])
(It is the largest coordinated software cutover in history up to that point and it works almost flawlessly. On the same day, ARPANET is split in two: military traffic moves to a separate network called **MILNET**, and the civilian-research half eventually becomes the public internet. //The "secret" half goes one way; the "open" half goes the other.//)
[footnote 1] Leiner, Barry M., et al. "Brief History of the Internet." Internet Society, 1997 (revised 2017).

'1983.3.23'
"Reagan Announces the Strategic Defense Initiative"
@importance 2
(In a televised address from the Oval Office, President Reagan calls for a space-based missile shield — quickly nicknamed **"Star Wars"** by the press.[footnote 1])
(SDI never produces a working anti-missile system, but it pours //billions of dollars// into computing, lasers, satellite communications, and software engineering. Many of the supercomputing and networking advances of the late 1980s — including early high-bandwidth backbone links — are funded as SDI side effects. The internet's adolescence is, again, paid for by the Cold War.)
[footnote 1] Reagan, Ronald. "Address to the Nation on Defense and National Security." March 23, 1983. Reagan Presidential Library archives.

'1989.3.12'
"Tim Berners-Lee Proposes the World Wide Web"
@importance 5
(At **CERN**, the European particle-physics lab in Geneva, British software engineer Tim Berners-Lee submits a paper titled //"Information Management: A Proposal"// to his manager, Mike Sendall.[footnote 1])
(Sendall famously writes "**vague but exciting**" on the cover and approves the project. Berners-Lee's proposal combines hypertext — an idea going back to Vannevar Bush in 1945 — with the existing TCP/IP internet to create a single global information system. Within two years he has built the world's first web server, web browser, and website.)
[footnote 1] Berners-Lee, Tim. "Information Management: A Proposal." CERN, March 1989. CERN Document Server.

'1991.8.6'
"First Public Webpage Goes Live"
@importance 4
(Berners-Lee posts the first publicly available webpage at //info.cern.ch//, explaining what the World Wide Web is and how to make a webpage of your own.[footnote 1])
(For the first time, the **military internet** of ARPA and the **academic internet** of NSFNET have a friendly, hyperlinked face. The Web is intentionally released without a patent — Berners-Lee has said he wanted it to belong to //everyone, like the alphabet//.)
<>
{ARTIFACT — Screenshot of info.cern.ch, the world's first webpage, c. 1992. Source: CERN; reconstructed from original NeXT machine archives.}
[footnote 1] Berners-Lee, Tim. "World Wide Web Project." info.cern.ch, August 6, 1991. Archived at https://www.w3.org/History/19921103-hypertext/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html.

'1995.4.30'
"NSFNET Shuts Down — Commercial Internet Era Begins"
@importance 4
(The **National Science Foundation Network**, the U.S. government's main internet backbone since 1986, is officially decommissioned.[footnote 1])
(Its traffic is handed off to commercial carriers — MCI, Sprint, and a new company called UUNET. After thirty years of being **paid for by the Pentagon**, the internet is now privately owned, privately operated, and open to commercial use. Amazon launches the same year. Google follows in 1998. //The military weapon has finished becoming the World Wide Web.//)
[footnote 1] National Science Foundation. "NSFNET — The Partnership That Changed the World." NSF, 2007.

'2025.1.13'
"U.S. Tightens AI Chip Export Controls"
@importance 3
(In its final week, the Biden administration issues the **AI Diffusion Interim Final Rule**, dividing every country in the world into three tiers and sharply restricting which nations can buy advanced Nvidia chips and have them shipped from American soil.[footnote 1])
(The Trump administration, taking office days later, keeps most of the framework in place. The result is the most aggressive U.S. attempt since the Cold War to **deny adversaries access to a strategic technology** — an explicit echo of 1950s CoCom export controls on Soviet computing. //The internet's hardware layer is, once again, a Cold War battleground.//)
[footnote 1] U.S. Department of Commerce, Bureau of Industry and Security. "Framework for Artificial Intelligence Diffusion." /Federal Register/, January 15, 2025. Reported in: Swanson, Ana, and Tripp Mickle. "Biden Issues Final Rule on Chip Exports." /The New York Times/, January 13, 2025.

'2025.8.27'
"Anthropic Reports Claude Misused for State-Linked Hacking"
@importance 3
(In a public **threat report**, Anthropic — the AI lab behind Claude — discloses that its models have been misused by what it describes as a //state-linked threat actor// to conduct cyber-espionage against U.S. tech, finance, and government targets.[footnote 1])
(For the first time, an American AI company is publicly framing its own product the way the Pentagon once framed ARPANET: a **dual-use technology** that has to be defended against the very adversaries it can be used against. The internet is once again a battlefield — only now the combatants are language models.)
[footnote 1] Anthropic. "Detecting and Countering Misuse of AI: August 2025." Anthropic Threat Report, August 27, 2025. Reported in: Roose, Kevin. "Anthropic Says Hackers Tried to Use Claude for Cyberattacks." /The New York Times/, August 27, 2025.
